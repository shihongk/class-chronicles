# S6: Invisible Understanding — JC Economics (Market Failure)

## Scenario Profile
**Level**: JC  
**Class Profile**: Invisible Understanding  
**Auto-derived**: Economics / Market failure and government intervention  

**Core tension**: Students can state the market failure and give the correct intervention, but their underlying reasoning is partial or invisible. The teacher must decide whether to push for explanation, surface misconceptions, or move on with the lesson. [file:1][file:4][file:5]

## Episode 1 — What you notice first

**Setup**:  
It is 25 minutes into a JC Economics lesson on market failure. You have introduced a negative externality diagram and asked students to explain why government intervention may be needed. As you circulate, you notice that several students can label the diagram correctly and can name a policy, but many cannot explain the link between the social cost curve, welfare loss, and the need for intervention. [file:5][file:6]

**What you see in class**:  
- Rajan writes the correct diagram and policy, but gives only one-line explanations.  
- Nur can describe the diagram shifts, but leaves out the welfare argument.  
- Wei Jie can state “tax is better,” but cannot justify why.  
- Siti has the right answer after discussion, but her written work shows little reasoning. [file:5][file:6]

**Decision point**: What do you do next, with 15 minutes left? [file:5][file:6]

### Choice A: Use a hinge question
Ask a short diagnostic question that tests whether students can connect the diagram to the welfare argument and the need for intervention. For example: “If marginal social cost is above marginal private cost, what does that tell us about the market outcome?” This gives you quick evidence of who understands the mechanism and who is only naming the right terms. [file:5][file:6]

### Choice B: Move into structured peer explanation
Put students into pairs and ask them to explain the same market failure using sentence stems: “The market fails because...”, “This creates...”, “Government should intervene by...” The goal is to surface hidden reasoning through talk and let students hear how a complete explanation sounds. [file:4][file:5]

### Choice C: Re-teach with a worked chain
Pause the class and model the full reasoning chain from diagram to welfare loss to intervention, then ask students to complete a guided set of prompts. This gives more support to students whose understanding is partial, but it reduces time for student articulation. [file:4][file:5]

## Episode 2 — Consequences

### If you chose A: Hinge question
The class response shows a split: about half can identify the correct intervention, but only a few can explain the welfare link clearly. You now know the issue is not just accuracy but invisible reasoning, so your next move must be shaped by that evidence. [file:5][file:6]

#### A1: Give targeted feedback and regroup
You briefly address the misconception, then regroup students based on need: one group works on the welfare chain, another works on applying the intervention to a new example. This keeps the lesson moving while using evidence to differentiate the next step. [file:4][file:5]

#### A2: Ask for written justification before moving on
Students write a two-sentence justification before you continue, so every student must make the reasoning visible. This gives you stronger evidence than oral answers alone, but it takes time and may slow the lesson. [file:5][file:6]

### If you chose B: Structured peer explanation
The pair talk reveals that many students can sound confident when prompted, but their explanations are still incomplete or repetitive. You now have evidence that talk alone is not enough unless it is tightly structured and followed by individual accountability. [file:5][file:6]

#### B1: Collect a revised written response
After the peer talk, students rewrite their answer individually, using the best explanation they heard. This makes the feedback cycle visible: talk first, then written evidence of learning. [file:5][file:6]

#### B2: Publicly compare two student responses
You show one partial answer and one fuller answer, then ask the class to identify what is missing and why it matters. This makes the invisible thinking more explicit, but it works best if the class is comfortable with discussion and critique. [file:5][file:6]

### If you chose C: Worked chain
The class becomes quieter and more secure, but the stronger students are now mostly following rather than thinking ahead. You have reduced uncertainty, but you still need to check whether students can independently reproduce the logic. [file:4][file:5]

#### C1: Follow with a quick retrieval check
Ask students to apply the same chain to a new market failure example, such as positive externalities or demerit goods. This checks whether the re-teach has actually changed understanding rather than just producing short-term compliance. [file:5][file:6]

#### C2: Turn the worked example into a completion task
Give students a partly completed explanation and ask them to fill in the missing links. This keeps support high while still making students do some of the cognitive work themselves. [file:4][file:5]

## Episode 3 — Outcome and reflection

### Outcome after A1
Students are more evenly grouped by need, and you have clearer evidence of who understands the welfare mechanism and who does not. The lesson ends with a stronger link between assessment evidence and differentiated follow-up, but the class may still need more time to consolidate the explanation chain. [file:4][file:5]

### Outcome after A2
You get good written evidence, but the lesson becomes less fluid and students may experience the task as more demanding than before. This is useful when the invisible thinking is important, but it may not be the best choice if time is very limited. [file:5][file:6]

### Outcome after B1
Students improve their written explanations after hearing peer models, which shows that the hidden reasoning can be surfaced through talk and rewriting. The risk is that some students borrow language without fully understanding it. [file:5][file:6]

### Outcome after B2
The class can see the difference between a partial and a complete explanation, which helps make the invisible reasoning visible. The trade-off is that weaker students may still need more scaffolding to produce such explanations on their own. [file:5][file:6]

### Outcome after C1
Students have a second chance to prove understanding independently, and you can see who can transfer the reasoning to a new case. This is strong assessment for learning, but it may still leave some students needing more guided support. [file:5][file:6]

### Outcome after C2
The completion task reduces cognitive load and helps more students participate, but it may not fully reveal whether they can build the reasoning chain independently. It is a good option when the goal is to balance support and access in the same lesson. [file:4][file:5]

**Reflection framing**: What does it mean for a student to be “correct” if the reasoning is still invisible? How can assessment, feedback, and differentiation work together so that hidden understanding becomes visible without turning the lesson into a lecture? [file:4][file:5][file:6]

## Theory anchors
- **Assessment for learning**: use student responses as evidence to decide the next step. [file:5][file:6]
- **Differentiated instruction**: adjust support, grouping, or task structure based on what students show. [file:4][file:7]
- **Feedback**: make the gap between partial and complete reasoning visible. [file:5][file:6]
- **STP lesson enactment**: respond to student thinking in the moment rather than relying only on pre-planned explanation. [file:7][file:8]
